# Episode 5a — Covenant Renewed and the Greatest Passover

---

## Ages 6–9 — The Biggest Party for God

Josiah did not keep the Book of the Law to himself.

He called everyone together.

Leaders.
Priests.
Mothers and fathers.
Children.
People from Jerusalem and from towns all across Judah.

They gathered at the temple.

Josiah stood beside a large pillar and began to read every word from God’s Book out loud. The people listened carefully. They heard about God’s promises. They heard about His commands. They heard about His faithfulness.

When Josiah finished reading, he made a promise.

“We will follow the Lord,” he said, “with all our heart.”

And the people agreed.

But Josiah did not stop with words.

He made sure every false god was removed. Workers searched the land again. They broke what had been rebuilt. They cleaned what had been hidden. Josiah even traveled far away to a place called Bethel and destroyed an old altar that had led people into sin many years before.

No idols were left.

Then Josiah planned something joyful.

A Passover celebration.

Families gathered together. Lambs were prepared. Priests and Levites worked carefully and happily. Songs were sung. The people remembered how God rescued their ancestors from slavery in Egypt.

The Bible says there had not been a Passover like this since the days of the judges.

It was the biggest celebration for God the nation had seen in a very long time.

Josiah loved God with his whole heart.

And the people rejoiced together.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- Promising God something should lead to action.
- God deserves our whole heart.
- Worship can be joyful.
- Celebrating what God has done strengthens faith.

> ### Think About It
> 1) Why did Josiah read the whole book out loud?
> 2) Why did he remove every idol before celebrating?
> 3) How can you celebrate God this week?

> ### Short Prayer
> “God, help me love You with my whole heart. Thank You for rescuing me. Amen.”

## Ages 10–13 — Covenant Stand + Greatest Passover

Josiah understood that rediscovering the Law was not enough.

It had to be obeyed.

He gathered the entire nation at the temple — leaders, priests, Levites, families, and even people from the former northern territories. Standing by a pillar, he read every word of the covenant aloud (2 Kings 23:2–3, KJV).

He did not summarize it.
He did not skip the hard parts.

Then he made a public covenant:

To walk after the Lord.
To keep His commandments.
To follow Him with all his heart and soul.

And the people stood with him.

After the vow came action.

Josiah completed the destruction of idolatry. He removed pagan priests. He defiled Topheth so child sacrifice would never happen there again. He tore down the altar at Bethel — the very altar Jeroboam had built generations earlier that led Israel into rebellion.

That altar had stood for over 300 years.

Josiah reduced it to dust.

Then came the Passover.

It was not routine.
It was renewal.

Priests were sanctified.
Levites instructed the people.
Sacrifices were prepared in abundance.

Families remembered the night God delivered Israel from Egypt.

Scripture records that there had been no Passover like it since the days of Samuel.

The nation had wandered for generations.

But for a moment, they stood united in covenant obedience.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- Covenant commitment requires public obedience.
- Partial reform leaves room for relapse.
- Remembering God’s rescue strengthens faith.
- Wholehearted devotion affects a whole community.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Why was it important to read the entire covenant publicly?
> 2) Why did Josiah destroy Bethel’s altar?
> 3) What would “all your heart” obedience look like for you?

> ### Short Prayer
> “Lord, help me follow You fully, not halfway.”

## Young Adult / Adult — All-In Covenant, Historic Renewal

The rediscovered Law demanded response.

Josiah assembled the nation and read “all the words of the book of the covenant” (2 Kings 23:2, KJV). The act itself was theological.

The king stood under the authority of Scripture.

He did not legislate reform apart from the Word.
He anchored reform in it.

He then entered into covenant publicly — echoing Deuteronomy 6:5 — to love the Lord with all heart, soul, and might.

This was not emotional zeal.
It was covenant re-alignment.

Josiah’s purge intensified.

Topheth was desecrated, ending ritual child sacrifice.
Idolatrous priests were removed.
High places were dismantled.
The altar at Bethel — first erected by Jeroboam in 1 Kings 12 — was destroyed in fulfillment of prophecy spoken centuries earlier (1 Kings 13).

This was not symbolic cleanup.

It was theological correction.

The Passover that followed marked the climax of his reform.

Passover remembers deliverance.
It centers identity on redemption.
It roots obedience in rescue.

Scripture’s verdict is stunning:

“There was no king before him, that turned to the LORD with all his heart, and with all his soul, and with all his might… neither after him arose there any like him” (2 Kings 23:25, KJV).

Josiah represents wholehearted covenant devotion.

Yet even here, the narrative holds tension.

Revival under one leader does not erase generational consequence.

But for a season, Judah experienced clarity.

Word restored.
Idols removed.
Covenant renewed.
Worship unified.

This is what happens when repentance becomes obedience.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- Scripture must govern reform.
- Wholehearted devotion requires decisive removal of compromise.
- Redemption fuels obedience.
- Legacy is shaped by covenant alignment, not charisma.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Where is obedience still partial in your life?
> 2) What long-standing “altars” need dismantling?
> 3) Is your worship rooted in remembrance of rescue?

# Episode 3 — The Lost Book in God’s House

---

## Ages 6–9 — The Lost Book in God’s House

King Josiah had already torn down idols across the land. Altars had fallen. Wooden poles had been chopped down. Statues had been crushed into dust.

But now he turned his eyes toward something different.

The temple.

God’s house stood in Jerusalem, but it did not look beautiful anymore. Stones were cracked. Doors hung crooked. Dust covered the floors. Years of neglect had left it tired and broken.

Josiah wanted to fix it.

He chose leaders he could trust. He sent Shaphan the scribe and other officials to collect money and pay workers. Builders carried stones. Carpenters shaped wood. Craftsmen repaired what had been damaged.

As they cleaned and restored, something unexpected happened.

In a quiet corner of the temple, the high priest Hilkiah found a scroll.

It was not just an old paper.

It was the Book of the Law — God’s Word given long ago through Moses.

For years, the people had lived without hearing it read. They had forgotten its commands. They had ignored its warnings.

Hilkiah gave the scroll to Shaphan.

Shaphan read it.

Then he brought it to the king.

When Shaphan began reading God’s words out loud, the room grew still. Josiah listened carefully. The more he heard, the more he understood how far the nation had wandered.

God’s Word had been lost.

But now it was found.

And everything was about to change.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- God’s Word is precious and powerful.
- Fixing the outside is not enough — hearts must listen too.
- God speaks through the Bible.
- It is never too late to return to Him.

> ### Think About It
> 1) How would you feel if you found a very special book that had been missing?
> 2) Why do you think the people had forgotten God’s Word?
> 3) How can you make sure you don’t “lose” God’s Word in your life?

> ### Short Prayer
> “Lord, thank You for the Bible. Help me listen carefully and obey like Josiah. Amen.”

## Ages 10–13 — The Scroll in the Dust

By the eighteenth year of his reign, Josiah had already begun reforming the land. Idols had been torn down. High places destroyed. But he understood something important:

Removing evil was not enough.
True worship required restoration.

The temple had suffered decades of misuse. Previous kings had allowed idols inside its courts. Sacred spaces were neglected. Worship had become shallow and scattered.

Josiah sent trusted officials to oversee repairs. Money was collected faithfully. Skilled workers restored beams, stones, and doors.

Then history shifted.

Hilkiah the high priest found a scroll buried in the temple. It was identified as the Book of the Law of the Lord given by Moses (2 Kings 22:8, KJV).

This was not merely religious literature.

It was covenant.

Commands.
Blessings.
Warnings.
Consequences.

It had been ignored long enough to be forgotten.

Hilkiah handed it to Shaphan. Shaphan read it privately — and then brought it before the king.

After reporting on the repairs, he added one sentence that changed everything:

“Hilkiah the priest hath delivered me a book.”

And he read it aloud.

As the words filled the room, Josiah heard promises of blessing for obedience — and severe warnings for rebellion. He realized Judah had broken covenant again and again.

The temple had been repaired.

But the nation’s heart had not.

The rediscovered scroll became a mirror. It revealed drift. It exposed disobedience. It awakened urgency.

God’s Word had not changed.

The people had.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- God’s Word defines truth, even when culture forgets it.
- Repairing structures does not replace restoring hearts.
- Scripture reveals both promise and warning.
- Revival begins when we truly hear God’s Word.

> ### Reflection
> 1) What do you think shocked Josiah most about the scroll?
> 2) Why is it dangerous to neglect Scripture?
> 3) Where might God’s Word be “buried” under distraction in your life?

> ### Short Prayer
> “God, don’t let Your Word gather dust in my life. Help me open it, hear it, and obey it.”

## Young Adult / Adult — When Covenant Is Rediscovered

Josiah’s eighteenth year marks one of the most significant rediscoveries in biblical history.

The temple was being restored structurally when the greater crisis was uncovered spiritually.

Hilkiah found “the book of the law in the house of the LORD” (2 Kings 22:8, KJV).

How does covenant disappear inside the covenant people?

Not through one act of rebellion —
but through gradual neglect.

Generations had worshiped without Scripture shaping them. Ritual remained, but revelation had faded. The temple stood, but its governing authority had been silenced.

The rediscovery of the Law exposed a devastating reality:

Judah had not merely drifted culturally.
She had violated covenant.

When Shaphan read the scroll before Josiah, the king recognized what others had normalized. Blessings had been promised for obedience. Curses had been declared for rebellion (Deuteronomy 28).

The exile of the northern kingdom was not random.
It was covenant consequence.

Judah was heading the same direction.

This moment reveals something profound:

Revival is not innovation.
It is rediscovery.

Josiah did not create new theology.
He returned to written truth.

The physical restoration of the temple had prepared the stage.
But the reading of the Word pierced the heart.

External reform without Scriptural authority would have faded.

The scroll reintroduced accountability.

And accountability demands response.

The rediscovery of Scripture did not comfort Josiah.

It convicted him.

And conviction would lead to the next decisive act.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- Spiritual decline often begins with neglect of Scripture.
- Ritual without revelation becomes hollow.
- Rediscovering God’s Word exposes both hope and warning.
- True revival is rooted in returning to covenant truth.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Has Scripture become background noise rather than governing authority?
> 2) Where might neglect be shaping your spiritual drift?
> 3) What would change if God’s Word became central again in your decisions?

# Episode 1 — Chosen but Hiding

---

## Ages 6–9 — Chosen by God, But Afraid

Long before David became king, Israel asked for something new.

They wanted a king like the other nations.

They didn’t want judges anymore.
They didn’t want prophets leading them.
They wanted someone tall, strong, and impressive.

God told the prophet Samuel to anoint a man named Saul.

Saul was exactly what people imagined a king should look like. The Bible says he stood head and shoulders taller than everyone else. He looked strong. He looked royal.

But when the day came for Samuel to present the new king to the people, something surprising happened.

Saul was missing.

The families of Israel gathered. Lots were cast to show whom God had chosen. The tribe of Benjamin was selected. Then Saul’s family. Then Saul himself.

But he was not standing in front of the crowd.

He was hiding among the supplies.

The tall warrior.
The chosen king.
Hiding behind baggage.

He didn’t feel ready.
He didn’t feel brave.
He may have felt small inside, even if he looked big outside.

The people asked Samuel, “Where is he?”

And the Lord answered clearly:
“Behold, he hath hid himself among the stuff.” (1 Samuel 10:22, KJV)

God knew exactly where Saul was.

They brought him out. He stood tall above the people. They shouted, “God save the king!”

Saul became king that day — not because he was fearless, but because God had chosen him.

Sometimes the first step of leadership is simply stepping out from where you are hiding.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- God sees you, even when you feel afraid.
- You can look strong on the outside and still feel nervous inside.
- When God calls you, He will help you step forward.
- Courage begins with obedience.

> ### Think About It
> 1) Why do you think Saul hid?
> 2) Have you ever felt nervous to do something important?
> 3) What helps you take a brave step?

## Ages 10–13 — Called, But Not Confident

Israel’s request for a king was complicated.

They wanted to be like the surrounding nations. They wanted visible leadership. God warned them through Samuel that a king would take sons for battle, daughters for service, and resources for himself.

But they insisted.

So God gave them Saul.

Saul looked the part. He came from a respected family. He was physically impressive. When Samuel privately anointed him, Saul responded with humility.

Yet when the public moment arrived, insecurity surfaced.

The tribes were gathered.
Lots were cast.
God revealed Saul.

And Saul was hiding among the baggage.

That detail is revealing.

He had been anointed.
He had received confirmation.
He had been told God would be with him.

And still, he tried to avoid the spotlight.

Calling does not eliminate insecurity.

But insecurity, if unmanaged, can shape leadership decisions.

Saul’s story begins with hesitation.

The real question is not whether fear exists.
It is whether fear governs.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- God’s calling is not based on your confidence.
- Fear can make people avoid responsibility.
- External appearance does not equal internal readiness.
- Stepping forward is often the first act of leadership.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Why do you think Israel wanted a king so badly?
> 2) Why would someone chosen by God still hide?
> 3) What responsibility might you be avoiding?

## Young Adult / Adult — The Tension Between Calling and Insecurity

Saul’s coronation is layered with theological irony.

Israel demanded a king “like all the nations.” In granting their request, God both honored their choice and exposed their motive.

Saul embodied what they wanted:
Height.
Presence.
Military potential.
Visibility.

Externally, he satisfied expectation.

Internally, he wrestled with insecurity.

When publicly identified as king, Saul hid among the baggage — a symbolic image that will echo throughout his reign.

He does not openly reject the throne.
He quietly avoids it.

This is the first insight into his leadership psychology.

Insecurity does not always rebel.
It retreats.

God’s calling collided with Saul’s self-perception.

He had already been anointed privately in 1 Samuel 9.
He had received signs confirming God’s choice.
He had been told the Spirit of the Lord would come upon him.

Yet when affirmation became public, fear surfaced.

This tension becomes a defining pattern:

- He will later fear public opinion.
- He will fear losing approval.
- He will fear David’s popularity.
- He will fear losing the kingdom.

The root appears here.

Leadership begins not with strength, but with identity.

Saul’s early humility is commendable.
But humility that turns into insecurity can become unstable.

God knew where Saul was hiding.
But Saul did not yet know who he was in God.

And that internal uncertainty will shape the arc of his reign.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- External gifting does not resolve internal insecurity.
- God may grant what we request, even when our motives are mixed.
- Avoidance is often fear in disguise.
- Identity confusion can distort leadership over time.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Where are you hiding behind competence or appearance?
> 2) Is fear quietly shaping your leadership decisions?
> 3) Do you trust God’s calling more than your self-assessment?

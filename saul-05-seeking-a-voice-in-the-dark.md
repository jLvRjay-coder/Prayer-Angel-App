# Episode 5 — Seeking a Voice in the Dark

---

## Ages 6–9 — Looking for Answers the Wrong Way

The Philistine army was gathering again.

Their tents covered the hills.
Their soldiers stood ready.

King Saul was afraid.

He tried to ask God what to do.
But he did not receive an answer.

Saul had made many wrong choices before this.
He had not listened when God corrected him.
Now heaven felt quiet.

Instead of turning back to God with a humble heart,
Saul made a very dangerous choice.

He went looking for a woman who spoke to spirits —
something God had clearly said was wrong.

Saul even disguised himself.
He went at night.

He did not want anyone to know.

Saul was searching for answers.
But he was searching in the wrong place.

A long time earlier, Saul had removed people like this from Israel.
Now he was secretly visiting one.

Fear had pushed him into darkness.

When we feel scared or confused,
it can be tempting to look for help anywhere.

But God wants us to come to Him —
even when the answer is hard,
even when we must say sorry first.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- Fear can lead to bad choices.
- Silence does not mean God is gone.
- Going secretly is often a warning sign.
- We should seek God first.

> ### Think About It
> 1) What do you do when you feel afraid?
> 2) Why is it important to obey even when it’s hard?
> 3) How can you remember to ask God for help first?

## Ages 10–13 — Desperation Without Repentance

Earlier in his reign, Saul had removed mediums and spiritists from the land.
He knew God’s command clearly.

But now, facing war and silence from heaven,
Saul did something unthinkable.

“The Lord answered him not.”

That sentence carries weight.

God had spoken to Saul many times before.
Through Samuel.
Through correction.
Through warning.

But Saul had repeatedly resisted.

Now, when he wanted direction,
he received silence.

Silence is not always absence.
Sometimes it is consequence.

Instead of repenting,
instead of asking where he had gone wrong,
Saul searched for information.

He disguised himself and went at night to a medium in Endor.

The king who once enforced God’s boundaries
was now breaking them in secret.

Desperation without repentance leads to compromise.

Saul wanted guidance,
but he did not want surrender.

He wanted answers,
but not accountability.

This moment shows how far he had drifted.

Fear pushed him past conviction.
And silence exposed what years of disobedience had built.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- Silence from God is not permission to disobey.
- Knowing truth does not guarantee obedience.
- Desperation can lead to spiritual shortcuts.
- Repentance should come before seeking direction.

> ### Reflection
> 1) What should we do when God feels silent?
> 2) Why is repentance sometimes harder than action?
> 3) Have you ever looked for answers without examining your heart?

## Young Adult / Adult — When Silence Reveals Separation

1 Samuel 28 is not simply a strange story about a medium.

It is the final exposure of Saul’s spiritual condition.

“The Lord answered him not.”

This is judicial silence.

Saul had rejected correction in chapter 13.
He redefined obedience in chapter 15.
He attacked God’s anointed in chapter 18.
He ruled from insecurity for years.

Now heaven is quiet.

Silence is terrifying when you are accustomed to control.

But Saul did not respond with brokenness.
He responded with substitution.

The king who once expelled mediums now seeks one.
The ruler who publicly enforced God’s law
privately violates it.

Notice the consistency of his pattern:

- When pressured, he rushed.
- When confronted, he justified.
- When threatened, he attacked.
- When silent heaven met him, he replaced God.

Saul did not lack revelation.
He lacked repentance.

He wanted strategy.
He wanted foresight.
He wanted certainty about tomorrow’s battle.

But he did not seek reconciliation.

There is a profound theological warning here:

When God’s voice is repeatedly resisted,
the heart grows less sensitive to it.

Silence becomes the mirror of accumulated refusal.

The tragedy is not that Saul consulted a medium.
The tragedy is that he did so without first falling on his face before God.

The battle of Gilboa was lost spiritually
before a single sword was raised.

Saul’s arc closes not in dramatic rebellion,
but in exhausted separation.

A man once chosen.
A king once anointed.
Now seeking guidance in darkness
because he would not walk in light.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- Repeated resistance dulls spiritual sensitivity.
- Silence should lead to repentance, not replacement.
- Desperation exposes misplaced trust.
- Authority without surrender eventually collapses.
- Spiritual decline is progressive, not sudden.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Where might you be seeking answers without surrender?
> 2) Has fear pushed you toward shortcuts?
> 3) If God feels silent, is there repentance He may be inviting?

# Episode 1 — Power Without God

---

## Ages 6–9 — A Queen Who Loved Being in Charge

### 🧭 Big Idea
Power without God can lead people the wrong way.

### 📜 The Story
Jezebel was a queen.

She lived in a palace with tall walls and shining rooms. Servants hurried when she spoke. Soldiers stood when she entered. People listened when she gave commands.

She liked being in charge.

Jezebel came from a land where people worshiped false gods. They bowed to idols made of stone and wood. They prayed to statues that could not hear.

When she married King Ahab of Israel, she brought those beliefs with her  
(1 Kings 16:31–33, KJV).

Israel was supposed to worship the Lord — the one true God.

But Jezebel did not want that.

She helped build altars for a false god named Baal. She encouraged people to stop worshiping the Lord. She filled the palace with new ways of thinking.

King Ahab listened to her.

Slowly, the land began to change.

Jezebel had power.

But she did not use it to lead people toward God.

She used it to get her own way.

And when power pushes God aside, it always brings trouble.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Power is meant to protect and guide, not control.
- Following false things leads people away from truth.
- Leaders influence many lives.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why do you think it matters what a leader believes?
> - How can you use your influence to help others choose what is right?

## Ages 10–13 — When Power Replaces Obedience

### 🧭 Big Idea
Leadership becomes dangerous when it answers to itself instead of to God.

### 📜 The Story
Jezebel was not unfamiliar with power.

She was the daughter of a king in Sidon. From childhood, she saw authority used boldly. In her homeland, worship of Baal was normal. Strength and control were admired.

When she married King Ahab of Israel, her influence did not remain private  
(1 Kings 16:31–33, KJV).

Israel had once been led by kings who knew the Lord. Imperfect kings, yes — but men who at least acknowledged God’s covenant.

Under Jezebel’s influence, that shifted.

Altars to Baal were built openly. Prophets of the Lord were silenced. Worship became confused. The palace no longer reflected obedience — it reflected preference.

Ahab did not resist her direction.

Scripture says he did “more to provoke the Lord God of Israel to anger than all the kings of Israel that were before him.”

This did not happen overnight.

It happened through influence.

Through slow compromise.
Through leadership that valued control over covenant.

Power without obedience does not stay neutral.

It redirects nations.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Influence shapes culture.
- Compromise often begins quietly.
- Leadership must remain accountable to God.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why is leadership dangerous when it ignores God?
> - How does compromise spread through influence?
> - Who influences the way you think and choose?

## Young Adult / Adult — Authority Without Accountability

### 🧭 Big Idea
Authority detached from submission to God becomes destructive — no matter how strong it appears.

### 📜 The Story
Jezebel enters Israel’s history not as a passive queen, but as a catalytic force.

Her marriage to Ahab was political — an alliance between Israel and Sidon. But alliances do not remain external. They reshape internal loyalties.

Through her, Baal worship gained institutional support  
(1 Kings 16:31–33, KJV).

Temples were built.
Altars erected.
Prophets of the Lord were hunted.

This was not ignorance.

It was strategy.

Jezebel believed authority justified direction. If she possessed position, she possessed the right to define truth. Her confidence did not come from covenant — it came from control.

That is the danger.

Biblical leadership is never autonomous. Kings in Israel were meant to rule under God, not instead of Him. They were stewards, not sovereigns.

Jezebel blurred that line.

She did not merely reject the Lord privately. She attempted to replace Him publicly.

And when authority answers only to itself, it inevitably moves toward suppression. Later episodes will show her silencing dissent, manipulating outcomes, and weaponizing power.

But the seed begins here.

Power without God.
Influence without accountability.
Confidence without submission.

Jezebel’s story opens with a warning that echoes beyond her generation:

Rejecting God does not remove His authority.

It removes protection.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Authority must remain under God’s authority.
- Cultural influence can either preserve truth or erode it.
- Power that defines its own morality becomes destructive.
- Leadership without accountability drifts toward control.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Who or what defines how you use the influence you’ve been given?
> - Where are you tempted to treat position as permission?
> - What safeguards keep your authority aligned with God?

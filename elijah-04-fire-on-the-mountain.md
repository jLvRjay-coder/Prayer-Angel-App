# Episode 4 — Fire on the Mountain

---

## Ages 6–9 — The God Who Answers by Fire

### 🧭 Big Idea
The Lord alone is God, and He answers those who trust Him.

### 📜 The Story
The drought had lasted a long time.

Fields were dry.
Wells were low.
The people were confused.

Some said they followed the Lord.
Some prayed to Baal.
Many tried to follow both.

So Elijah called everyone to Mount Carmel.

He stood before the people and asked:

“How long halt ye between two opinions? If the Lord be God, follow him: but if Baal, then follow him.”  
(1 Kings 18:21, KJV)

The people said nothing.

Elijah gave them a challenge.

Two altars.
Two sacrifices.
No fire.

“The God that answereth by fire, let him be God.”  
(1 Kings 18:24, KJV)

The prophets of Baal prayed first.

They shouted.
They danced.
They cried out from morning until noon.

But there was no voice.
No answer.
No fire.

Then Elijah repaired the broken altar of the Lord.

He used twelve stones — one for each tribe of Israel.

He placed the sacrifice on the wood.

Then he did something surprising.

He poured water over it.

Once.
Twice.
Three times.

Everything was soaked.

Then Elijah prayed.

Not long.
Not loud.

“Lord God of Abraham, Isaac, and of Israel… hear me.”  
(1 Kings 18:36–37, KJV)

Fire fell from heaven.

It burned the sacrifice.
The wood.
The stones.
Even the water.

The people fell on their faces and cried:

“The Lord, he is the God! The Lord, he is the God!”  
(1 Kings 18:39, KJV)

### 💡 What This Teaches
- God hears sincere prayer.
- We must choose whom we follow.
- God shows His power in His time.
- The Lord alone is God.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why did Elijah pour water on the altar?
> - What does it mean to choose God?
> - How do you know God listens when you pray?

## Ages 10–13 — When Truth Becomes Public

### 🧭 Big Idea
God does not compete with false gods — He reveals Himself.

### 📜 The Story
Mount Carmel was not a private moment.

It was a public confrontation.

Israel had been wavering —
trying to mix worship of the Lord with worship of Baal.

Elijah forced clarity.

“How long halt ye between two opinions?”

Wavering was not harmless.
It was divided loyalty.

The prophets of Baal cried out for hours.

They shouted louder.
They cut themselves.
They begged.

But Scripture says:

“There was neither voice, nor any to answer, nor any that regarded.”  
(1 Kings 18:29, KJV)

False gods demand effort.
They offer silence.

Then Elijah rebuilt the altar of the Lord — the one that had been broken down.

Twelve stones.
One nation.
One covenant.

He drenched the sacrifice with water.

No tricks.
No hidden spark.

Then he prayed a simple prayer asking that the people would know the Lord had turned their hearts back again.

Fire fell.

Not slowly.
Not partially.

Completely.

The people fell to the ground.

For a moment, truth was undeniable.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- We cannot serve two masters.
- God reveals truth clearly.
- Worship must be directed rightly.
- Public compromise requires public clarity.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why do people try to mix truth with compromise?
> - What does “limping between two opinions” look like today?
> - Where do you need clarity in your allegiance?

## Young Adult / Adult — Confronting Cultural Idolatry

### 🧭 Big Idea
When compromise becomes cultural norm, God exposes false foundations.

### 📜 The Story
1 Kings 18 is theological confrontation at scale.

Baal was believed to govern rain and fertility.
The drought was already a polemic against him.

Mount Carmel becomes courtroom.

Elijah’s question is diagnostic:

“How long halt ye between two opinions?”

Spiritual indecision is not neutrality.
It is instability.

The prophets of Baal represent performative religion.

Volume.
Intensity.
Self-inflicted spectacle.

Yet silence remains.

The text repeats the emptiness:

“No voice. No answer. No attention.”

False systems exhaust their followers because they have nothing to give.

Elijah’s approach is markedly different.

He rebuilds what had been broken.

The altar is restored before the fire falls.

Twelve stones — even in national division — remind Israel that covenant identity remains intact.

Then comes deliberate impossibility.

Water poured once.
Twice.
Three times.

The trench filled.

Any suspicion of manipulation is removed.

Elijah’s prayer is brief.

“Let it be known this day that thou art God in Israel… and that thou hast turned their heart back again.”  
(1 Kings 18:36–37, KJV)

The fire validates not Elijah’s charisma,
but God’s sovereignty.

Notice the sequence across Elijah’s life:

Hidden formation.
Daily dependence.
Sustained scarcity.
Then public confrontation.

Fire falls only after obedience.

Yet even this victory is not the end of struggle.

Mountaintop revelation does not eliminate future fear.

Miracles reveal power.
They do not perfect the prophet.

Still, on that mountain, the illusion collapsed.

The Lord — He is God.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Cultural idolatry must eventually be confronted.
- God exposes what false systems cannot produce.
- Obedience prepares the ground for revelation.
- Public victories do not replace ongoing dependence.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - What “Baals” compete for loyalty in your environment?
> - Are you wavering between comfort and conviction?
> - What altar in your life needs rebuilding before you seek fire?

# Episode 4 — A Tender Heart Before a Holy God

---

## Ages 6–9 — Josiah’s Sad but Brave Heart

Shaphan finished reading the scroll.

The room felt different now.

The words from God’s Book were not light or easy. They spoke about blessings for obedience. But they also warned about trouble if God’s people turned away.

As Josiah listened, his heart felt heavy.

The words described things Judah had done.
Idols.
Disobedience.
Ignoring God for many years.

Josiah understood something important.

This wasn’t just history.
This was about them.

He stood up quickly and tore his clothes. In those days, tearing your clothes was a way to show deep sadness and repentance.

“This is serious,” Josiah said. “We have not obeyed these words.”

He didn’t blame the people.
He didn’t defend himself.
He didn’t pretend it wasn’t true.

Instead, he sent his helpers to speak to a prophet named Huldah. She was a woman who spoke messages from God.

They asked her what the Lord would say about the scroll that had been found.

Huldah gave a clear answer.

God had seen the sin.
Judgment would come.

But she also said something hopeful.

“Because your heart was tender,” God said to Josiah, “and you humbled yourself before Me… I have heard you.”

Josiah’s repentance mattered.

He could not erase the past.
But he could respond rightly in the present.

And God saw his soft heart.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- God sees when we are truly sorry.
- Being brave sometimes means admitting wrong.
- Repentance is listening and responding.
- God cares about the condition of our hearts.

> ### Think About It
> 1) Why did Josiah tear his clothes?
> 2) Why is it brave to admit you were wrong?
> 3) How can you show God you are listening?

> ### Short Prayer
> “Lord, give me a soft heart. Help me listen when You speak and say sorry quickly. Amen.”

## Ages 10–13 — The Torn Clothes Moment

When Shaphan read the Book of the Law aloud, the atmosphere in the court shifted.

The words were unmistakable.

They described blessings for obedience — but also detailed consequences for rebellion. Drought. Defeat. Exile. These were not vague threats. They were covenant realities written long before.

And Judah had broken that covenant.

Josiah did not argue with the text.
He did not reinterpret it.
He did not shift blame to earlier kings.

He tore his clothes.

In the ancient world, this was not dramatic exaggeration. It was public humility. It meant: I accept responsibility. I grieve this truth.

“Great is the wrath of the LORD,” he said, recognizing that generations had ignored God’s commands.

Josiah sent leaders to inquire of Huldah the prophetess. Her message was firm.

Judgment would come.
The nation’s rebellion would not be erased.

But because Josiah’s heart was tender — because he humbled himself and wept before the Lord — he would not see the coming disaster.

One leader’s humility delayed judgment for a generation.

Josiah could not change what had already been written.
But he could change how he responded to it.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- Scripture reveals both promise and warning.
- Real repentance accepts truth without excuses.
- Leadership begins with personal humility.
- A tender heart can influence history.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Why didn’t Josiah argue with the scroll?
> 2) What does it mean to have a “tender heart”?
> 3) When Scripture challenges you, how do you respond?

> ### Short Prayer
> “God, when Your Word corrects me, help me respond with humility instead of excuses.”

## Young Adult / Adult — Tenderness Under Covenant Weight

When the Book of the Law was read, Josiah encountered more than ancient instruction.

He encountered covenant accountability.

The curses outlined in Deuteronomy aligned with Judah’s history with unsettling precision. The northern kingdom had already fallen. Assyria had already demonstrated what covenant judgment looked like.

Josiah recognized the pattern immediately.

He tore his garments — an embodied confession that the king himself stood under the authority of the text.

Notice what he did not do:

He did not dismiss the Law as outdated.
He did not reinterpret judgment as metaphor.
He did not shield himself behind political power.

He humbled himself.

This is the turning point.

Reform without repentance becomes performance.
Repentance without humility becomes emotion.

Josiah’s grief was covenantal awareness.

When he sent to Huldah, the prophetess confirmed both justice and mercy. Judgment would not be removed. The trajectory of the nation had been set by decades of rebellion.

But mercy was extended personally.

“Because thine heart was tender, and thou hast humbled thyself before the LORD… I also have heard thee” (2 Kings 22:19, KJV).

Tenderness is not weakness.
It is spiritual responsiveness.

Josiah’s repentance did not cancel national consequence.
But it altered timing.

His humility delayed disaster.
His posture modeled submission.
His response validated Scripture’s authority.

This moment reveals a profound truth:

The health of a nation may decline gradually,
but the health of a heart is decided immediately.

Judgment may loom historically.
Mercy is experienced personally.

Josiah could not rewrite Judah’s past.
But he refused to harden himself against truth.

And that mattered.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- Scripture stands above leadership.
- Repentance requires submission, not strategy.
- Tenderness is strength under conviction.
- Personal humility can alter generational timing.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Has familiarity with Scripture dulled your sense of its authority?
> 2) When confronted with truth, do you defend or submit?
> 3) What would it look like to respond immediately instead of gradually?

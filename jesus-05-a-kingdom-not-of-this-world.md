# Episode 5 — A Kingdom Not of This World

---

## Ages 6–9 — A Different Kind of King

### 🧭 Big Idea
Jesus is King — but not the way people expected.

### 📜 The Story
When Jesus rode into Jerusalem, the city felt alive.

Crowds gathered along the road.
They waved palm branches.
They shouted, “Hosanna! Blessed is the King!”

They believed the promised King had finally come.

But they imagined something very specific.

They wanted a king who would defeat Rome.
A king who would drive soldiers out.
A king who would take control.

Kings usually rode strong war horses.

Jesus rode a donkey.

It was quiet.
It was humble.
It was different.

Later, Jesus was taken before a Roman governor named Pilate.

Pilate asked Him, “Art thou the King of the Jews?”

Jesus answered, “My kingdom is not of this world.”

That did not mean He was not a king.

It meant His kingdom worked differently.

He did not come to take over cities.

He came to change hearts.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Jesus is truly King.
- God’s plans are bigger than political power.
- Jesus rules differently than earthly leaders.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why did the crowd expect a different kind of king?
> - What does it mean for Jesus to rule your heart?

## Ages 10–13 — Expectations and Allegiance

### 🧭 Big Idea
Jesus’ kingdom is built on truth and loyalty — not force.

### 📜 The Story
By the time Jesus entered Jerusalem, hope was rising.

The people were tired of Roman rule.
Taxes were heavy.
Soldiers walked their streets.

Many believed the Messiah would overthrow the empire.

When Jesus rode into the city, it looked like a royal procession.

Crowds shouted.
Branches covered the road.
Excitement filled the air.

But something was different.

He rode a donkey — a symbol of peace, not war.

He did not gather an army.
He did not plan rebellion.
Instead, He cleansed the temple and confronted religious leaders about hypocrisy.

Then came the trial.

Standing before Pilate, the Roman governor asked,  
“Art thou the King of the Jews?”

Jesus did not deny it.

But He redefined it.

“My kingdom is not of this world.”

If His kingdom were political, His followers would fight.

They did not.

His authority did not begin in Rome.
It did not depend on public approval.
It did not grow through violence.

This confused everyone.

Rome feared revolt.
Religious leaders feared loss of control.
The crowd feared disappointment.

They wanted rescue from Rome.

Jesus came to rescue from sin.

His kingdom required more than excitement.

It required allegiance.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Jesus’ mission was deeper than political freedom.
- Earthly power often uses force; Christ’s kingdom advances by truth.
- Following Jesus may challenge our expectations.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why do people often mix faith with political power?
> - What does it mean to give Jesus true allegiance?
> - Are you following Him for what He gives — or for who He is?

## Young Adult / Adult — Collision of Kingdoms

### 🧭 Big Idea
Christ’s kingdom confronts every earthly system because it is not sourced from them.

### 📜 The Story
The trial before Pilate reveals more than legal proceedings.

It reveals a collision of kingdoms.

Rome embodied imperial authority.
Caesar claimed divine honor.
The empire preserved order through dominance and fear.

Standing before that structure was Christ — bound, restrained, outwardly powerless.

Yet He spoke of a kingdom.

“My kingdom is not of this world.”

The statement is not escapism.

It is origin.

His authority does not derive legitimacy from earthly structures.
It does not rise through violence.
It does not secure influence through compromise.

It is sourced in heaven.

Pilate asked, “What is truth?”

The irony is striking.

Truth stood before him.

Throughout Israel’s history, kings had defended territory and expanded borders.

Some gained temporary victory.
All eventually fell.

Jesus did not come to expand land.

He came to reclaim humanity.

The crowd that shouted “Hosanna” days earlier would soon fall silent.

They expected confrontation with Rome.

Instead, they witnessed submission.

The cross would appear to be defeat.

It was enthronement.

Christ did not seize authority.

He revealed it through surrender.

He did not overthrow Rome with a sword.

He exposed the deeper tyranny of sin and defeated it through sacrifice.

A kingdom “not of this world” does not mean disconnected from this world.

It means not sourced by it.

It begins in conscience.
It reshapes loyalty.
It challenges every competing allegiance.

Religious systems cannot control it.
Political systems cannot co-opt it.
Cultural movements cannot redefine it.

Because it does not depend on them.

The King stood before Pilate looking defeated.

But the throne was already His.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Christ’s authority does not originate from political systems.
- Allegiance to Jesus transcends culture and nationality.
- Truth-based authority unsettles both religious and secular power.
- The cross is not the collapse of kingship — it is its revelation.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Where have you confused cultural influence with spiritual authority?
> - What loyalties compete with your allegiance to Christ?
> - If His kingdom is not of this world, how should that shape your priorities?

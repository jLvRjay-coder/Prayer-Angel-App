# Episode 2 — Smashing the Fake Gods

---

## Ages 6–9 — Smashing What Was Wrong

Josiah was no longer a little boy.

Years had passed since the crown was placed on his head. He had grown taller. His voice had grown stronger. But the most important change was inside him.

He loved the Lord.

When Josiah looked across Judah, he saw something that troubled him deeply.

On hills outside the cities, smoke rose from altars built to false gods. Wooden poles called Asherah stood tall in the ground. Carved statues lined courtyards. Shiny metal idols reflected the sunlight.

People bowed to things made of wood and stone.

Even in Jerusalem — the city where God’s temple stood — false worship had taken root.

Josiah could not ignore it.

In the twelfth year of his reign, he gave a bold command.

“Tear them down.”

Men went out with tools and ropes. Altars to Baal were broken apart. The Asherah poles were chopped down. Statues were smashed into pieces so small they became dust.

The dust was scattered — a reminder that fake gods cannot save.

Josiah did not stop at the city walls. He sent workers into towns across Judah. Even in places far to the north, where people had long forgotten the Lord, idols fell.

The land that had once been crowded with false worship began to look different.

Josiah understood something important:

If the people were going to follow God again, the false gods had to go.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- Loving God means removing what pulls us away from Him.
- Idols cannot help or save.
- Obedience sometimes requires courage.
- Cleaning out what is wrong makes room for what is right.

> ### Think About It
> 1) Why didn’t Josiah leave the idols alone?
> 2) What are things that can distract us from loving God?
> 3) Why is it brave to remove something that others think is normal?

## Ages 10–13 — The Twelfth Year Reform

Josiah had been seeking the Lord quietly for years. But private devotion eventually became public action.

In the twelfth year of his reign, he began to purge Judah and Jerusalem of high places, Asherah poles, carved images, and molten idols (2 Chronicles 34:3–7, KJV).

These were not harmless decorations.

High places represented alternative worship systems. Asherah poles symbolized fertility cults tied to surrounding nations. Baal altars stood as public declarations that another “god” controlled rain and harvest.

Josiah understood that divided worship weakens covenant identity.

So he acted decisively.

Altars were torn down in front of the people. Incense stands were cut apart. Idols were ground into powder and scattered on the graves of those who had sacrificed to them — a dramatic sign that false worship leads only to death.

His reform did not stop at Judah’s borders.

Josiah extended his efforts into the territories of Manasseh, Ephraim, Simeon, and even Naphtali — regions once part of the northern kingdom that had already fallen into exile.

This was not political expansion.

It was covenant restoration.

Before the Law was rediscovered in the temple, Josiah was already clearing the ground.

Reform often begins with removal.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- Divided loyalty weakens faith.
- Obedience sometimes requires visible action.
- Reform is not passive — it is intentional.
- Removing compromise prepares the way for renewal.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Why do you think Josiah acted before everyone agreed?
> 2) What “high places” might exist in culture today?
> 3) Why is it easier to manage compromise than remove it?

## Young Adult / Adult — Reform Before Revival

Josiah’s twelfth year marks the transition from private pursuit to structural reform.

Second Chronicles records that he began to purge Judah and Jerusalem of high places, Asherim, carved images, and molten idols (2 Chronicles 34:3–7, KJV).

This was not symbolic adjustment.

It was systematic demolition.

The high places represented normalized deviation from centralized worship. They had existed for generations. Many were culturally embedded and locally defended.

Josiah dismantled them anyway.

Altars were broken.
Images were crushed.
Sacred groves were cut down.

He ground idols to powder — a deliberate echo of Moses grinding the golden calf in Exodus 32. The message was unmistakable: false worship deserves obliteration, not accommodation.

His campaign extended into former northern territories. With Assyria’s dominance weakening, Josiah seized a rare political moment to restore covenant identity beyond Judah’s immediate borders.

Theologically, this was obedience to Deuteronomic command: rival worship must be removed entirely.

Politically, it was destabilizing.

Spiritually, it was preparatory.

Notice the sequence:

- Josiah sought the Lord.
- Then he removed idols.
- Only later would the Book of the Law be rediscovered.

Reform did not begin with information.
It began with conviction.

Josiah did not yet possess the rediscovered scroll, but he understood enough to know that compromise had to go.

Revival requires clearing ground.

You cannot build covenant faithfulness on foundations still occupied by rival allegiances.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- Renewal requires removal before restoration.
- Cultural compromise becomes invisible when normalized.
- Conviction must precede clarity.
- Reform is costly but necessary for revival.

> ### Reflection
> 1) What long-standing compromises feel “normal” in your environment?
> 2) Are you attempting to manage idols instead of dismantle them?
> 3) What ground must be cleared before deeper renewal can occur?

# Episode 6 — The King Who Took a Cross

---

## Ages 6–9 — The King Who Did Not Fight Back

### 🧭 Big Idea
Jesus chose the cross because He loves us.

### 📜 The Story
After Jesus was arrested, everything happened quickly.

Soldiers led Him away.
They mocked Him.
They placed a crown made of thorns on His head.

They laughed and said, “Hail, King of the Jews.”

But this was not a real crown.
It hurt.

They nailed Him to a wooden cross.

People watched.

Some shook their heads.
Some shouted.
Some cried.

If Jesus was truly King, why did He not stop it?

He had healed the sick.
He had calmed storms.
He had commanded demons.

He had the power.

But He did not come down from the cross.

He stayed.

Jesus was not weak.

He was willing.

He was taking the punishment for sin — not His own, but ours.

When He said, “It is finished,” He was not giving up.

He was completing something.

The price for sin had been paid.

The King had chosen the cross.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- Jesus chose the cross on purpose.
- He took punishment we deserved.
- Real strength sometimes looks quiet.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why do you think Jesus did not fight back?
> - What does it mean that He took our place?

## Ages 10–13 — The Cost of Redemption

### 🧭 Big Idea
The cross was not an accident — it was God’s rescue plan.

### 📜 The Story
The crucifixion did not surprise Jesus.

He had warned His disciples.
Prophets had written about it long before.

Isaiah said He would be despised.
Pierced.
Bruised for our iniquities.

Roman crucifixion was brutal.

It was public.
It was humiliating.
It was meant to crush rebellion.

Above His head, a sign was placed:

“King of the Jews.”

It was meant as mockery.

But it spoke truth.

At the cross, two realities collided:

Human injustice.
Divine purpose.

Religious leaders condemned Him.
Rome executed Him.
The crowd turned away.

But none of it was outside God’s plan.

Jesus could have called angels.

He did not.

He surrendered.

The cross was not a mistake in the mission.

It was the mission.

He bore sin.
He carried judgment.
He stood in our place.

Sacrifice accomplished what force never could.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- The cross fulfilled God’s promise.
- Jesus willingly took our punishment.
- God can bring redemption through suffering.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Why was the cross necessary?
> - How does knowing it was planned change how you see it?
> - What does sacrifice teach about love?

## Young Adult / Adult — The Paradox of the Throne

### 🧭 Big Idea
The cross reveals true kingship — authority expressed through substitution.

### 📜 The Story
The crucifixion is the paradox at the center of Christianity.

The promised King was crowned — with thorns.
Enthroned — on wood.
Exalted — through humiliation.

Every earthly system interpreted it as failure.

Rome saw execution.
Religious leaders saw elimination.
The crowd saw collapse.

Heaven saw substitution.

Isaiah declared, “He was wounded for our transgressions… and with his stripes we are healed.”

Paul later wrote, “He hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin.”

The cross exposes something deeper than injustice.

It reveals transaction.

Justice requires payment.
Holiness cannot ignore rebellion.
Sin carries consequence.

If God overlooked sin, He would not be just.
If He judged without mercy, humanity would be undone.

At the cross, justice and mercy converged.

The King bore what subjects deserved.

Not as helpless victim.

As willing substitute.

Throughout Israel’s history, kings protected themselves.

Saul grasped for approval.
Ahab manipulated truth.
Herod killed to secure position.

Christ relinquished safety.

The soldiers gambled for His garments, unaware that history was pivoting beneath their feet.

Pilate questioned truth while Truth hung before him.

The crowd demanded spectacle.

He gave obedience.

When Jesus said, “It is finished,” the word meant completed.

The debt was paid.
The work accomplished.
The sacrifice accepted.

The cross is not primarily about pain.

It is about reconciliation.

Worldly power destroys enemies.

The King died for His.

This is why His kingdom is not of this world.

It conquers not by killing rebellion,
but by absorbing it.

### 💡 What This Teaches
- The cross is substitution, not tragedy.
- Justice and mercy meet in Christ.
- Divine strength is revealed through surrender.
- True kingship absorbs cost rather than avoiding it.

> ### 🪑 Sit With This
> - Where are you still trying to earn what Christ finished?
> - How does substitution reshape your understanding of grace?
> - If the King gave Himself for you, what does allegiance require?

# Episode 2 — A King in a Hurry

---

## Ages 6–9 — When Waiting Feels Scary

The enemy army was gathering.

Dust rose in the distance.
Chariots rattled.
Shields flashed in the sun.

The Philistines looked strong.
Israel looked small.

Saul’s soldiers were nervous.
Some whispered.
Some slipped quietly away.

Samuel had given Saul clear instructions:
“Wait seven days. I will come. We will seek the Lord first.”

Saul waited.

Day one.
Day two.
Day three.

By day seven, fear was louder than faith.

Samuel was not there yet.

Saul counted the soldiers again.
Fewer.
And fewer.

“What if Samuel doesn’t come?”
“What if we lose?”
“What if everyone leaves?”

Waiting began to feel dangerous.

So Saul made a decision.

Instead of waiting for Samuel, he offered the sacrifice himself.

The smoke had barely lifted into the air when Samuel arrived.

“What have you done?” Samuel asked.

Saul tried to explain.
“The people were scattering.”
“You didn’t come on time.”
“The enemy was ready.”

But Samuel said something serious:

“You have not kept the command of the Lord.”

Saul wanted victory.

But he did not want to wait for it.

And sometimes the hardest part of obedience is trusting God when nothing feels safe.

### What This Teaches (6–9)
- Waiting can feel scary, but God is still in control.
- Fear can make us rush.
- Doing something fast is not the same as doing what is right.
- Obeying God matters more than looking strong.

> ### Think About It
> 1) Have you ever rushed because you were nervous?
> 2) Why can waiting feel harder than acting?
> 3) How can you remember to trust God when you feel pressure?

## Ages 10–13 — The Danger of Acting Too Soon

The Philistines were prepared.
Israel was not.

Scripture says the enemy had thousands of chariots and soldiers “as the sand which is on the sea shore” (1 Samuel 13:5, KJV).

Israel trembled.

Samuel had told Saul to wait seven days at Gilgal.

Saul did wait.
But he watched his army shrink as fear spread.

When Samuel did not arrive exactly when Saul expected, Saul decided he could not afford to delay.

So he crossed a line.

He offered the burnt offering himself — stepping into a priestly role God had not assigned to him.

It looked responsible.
It looked decisive.
It may have even looked spiritual.

But obedience is not measured by urgency.
It is measured by alignment.

The moment Saul finished, Samuel arrived.

The timing is painful.

Saul had almost obeyed.

When confronted, Saul defended himself:
- “The people were scattered.”
- “You came not within the days appointed.”
- “The Philistines gathered themselves together.”
- “I forced myself.”

Fear rewrote his reasoning.

This is the first fracture in Saul’s reign.

He did not deny God.
He did not reject sacrifice.
He did not mock the prophet.

He simply decided that God’s timing could bend to his pressure.

And that is where drift begins.

### What This Teaches (10–13)
- Pressure reveals what we truly trust.
- Almost obedience is still disobedience.
- Fear can make wrong choices sound reasonable.
- Leadership requires restraint as much as action.

> ### Reflection
> 1) What happens to your decision-making when pressure rises?
> 2) Have you ever justified something because it felt urgent?
> 3) What would trusting God’s timing look like for you right now?

## Young Adult / Adult — When Urgency Replaces Surrender

1 Samuel 13 marks the decisive internal shift in Saul’s leadership.

The instruction to wait was not logistical.
It was theological.

Waiting was the test.

Saul had already been anointed.
He had already been affirmed publicly.
He had already led an early military victory.

But now God tested whether Saul would govern through surrender or through control.

The battlefield pressure was real:
- Military disadvantage
- Declining morale
- A visible prophet delay
- Public expectation

Saul’s environment screamed for action.

Yet covenant leadership demands alignment, not improvisation.

By offering the sacrifice himself, Saul blurred the boundary between king and priest — between authority and submission.

This was not ignorance.
Saul knew the instruction.

It was anxiety-driven override.

His explanation reveals a pattern that will define him:

“I saw…”
“I said…”
“I forced myself…”

The language centers on self-perception, not divine trust.

Fear often disguises itself as responsibility.
Urgency often masquerades as leadership.

Saul still believed in God.
He still desired victory.
He still wanted divine favor.

But he wanted control more.

Samuel’s rebuke is severe:

“Thou hast done foolishly… thy kingdom shall not continue.”

The crown did not fall that day.
But the future shifted.

This was not about a single sacrifice.

It was about posture.

From this point forward, Saul will repeatedly:
- React to pressure
- Manage perception
- Protect image
- Override instruction

Leadership rarely collapses in one rebellion.
It erodes through repeated rationalizations.

The tragedy of Saul is not that he felt fear.

It is that he let fear govern obedience.

Waiting exposes who we trust.

And Saul revealed that he trusted visible stability more than invisible sovereignty.

### What This Teaches (Adult)
- Waiting is often God’s refining fire for leaders.
- Fear can subtly replace surrender.
- Spiritual authority requires respecting God’s boundaries.
- Urgency must never outrun obedience.
- Control is the counterfeit of trust.

> ### Reflection
> 1) Where are you “forcing yourself” instead of surrendering?
> 2) What outcome are you trying to secure apart from obedience?
> 3) If God delayed today, would your trust hold?

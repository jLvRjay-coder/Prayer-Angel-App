# Episode 4 — The King Who Touched the Untouchable

---

## Ages 6–9 — A King Who Came Close

Some people in Jesus’ day were not allowed to come near others.

If someone had a skin disease, they had to stay outside the town.

They had to shout, “Unclean!” so people would not get too close.

No one touched them.

One day, a man with leprosy came to Jesus.

He did not stay far away.
He knelt.

“If You are willing,” he said, “You can make me clean.”

Jesus did something surprising.

He reached out His hand.

He touched him.

No one else would.

“I am willing,” Jesus said.

And the man was healed.

Jesus did not step back from broken people.

He stepped toward them.

### What This Teaches (6–9)

• Jesus is not afraid of brokenness.  
• God cares about people others avoid.  
• True kindness moves toward those in need.  

### Reflection

Why do you think Jesus touched the man?
How can you show kindness to someone who feels left out?

## Ages 10–13 — Crossing the Line Others Drew

In ancient Israel, certain illnesses made a person ceremonially unclean.

That meant separation.

Distance.
Isolation.
Silence.

The leper who approached Jesus broke social rules.

He risked rejection.

But he believed something about Jesus:

“If You are willing…”

Jesus could have healed him with a word.

He had healed others at a distance before.

But this time, He touched him.

That touch mattered.

Under the law, contact with uncleanness made a person unclean.

But with Jesus, the opposite happened.

His holiness moved outward.

Instead of becoming defiled, He restored.

Later, a woman who had been bleeding for twelve years touched the edge of His garment.

She had lived in quiet isolation.
She had spent everything on doctors.

She did not want attention.

She just wanted healing.

Power left Him.

But instead of scolding her, He stopped.

He called her “Daughter.”

Compassion was not weakness.

It was authority applied personally.

### What This Teaches (10–13)

• Jesus’ authority restores instead of excludes.  
• Compassion sometimes crosses cultural boundaries.  
• Holiness in Christ moves outward to heal.  

### Reflection

Why did Jesus stop and speak to the woman publicly?
How do we treat people society overlooks?

## Young Adult / Adult — Authority That Moves Toward Defilement

The miracles of Jesus are not random acts of kindness.

They are theological statements.

Lepers represented visible decay.
Bleeding represented hidden suffering.
Demoniacs represented spiritual oppression.

Each category was marginalized.

Each carried stigma.

In a culture deeply shaped by purity laws, separation preserved order.

Distance protected reputation.

But Christ consistently reversed direction.

He moved toward contamination.

Not recklessly.
Not ignorantly.

Deliberately.

Under the Mosaic system, impurity spreads by contact.

In Christ, purity spreads by presence.

This is not lawlessness.
It is fulfillment.

He does not abolish holiness.
He embodies it.

The touch of Christ is not sentimental imagery.

It is confrontation with decay.

Where earthly kings guarded image, Jesus absorbed weakness.

Where rulers avoided shame, Christ entered it.

He did not fear being misunderstood.
He did not protect status.
He did not insulate Himself from suffering.

The leper’s restoration was physical.

The woman’s restoration was relational.

The demoniac’s restoration was spiritual.

Each act declared the same truth:

The King’s authority is not threatened by brokenness.

It overwhelms it.

Compassion, in Christ, is not emotional fragility.

It is strength under control.

The kings of Israel had often used power to preserve position.

Christ used power to restore the powerless.

He did not climb above the unclean.

He stepped into their space.

And in doing so, He revealed the nature of His kingdom:

Not separation for superiority.

But holiness that heals.

### What This Teaches (Adult)

• Divine authority moves toward brokenness, not away from it.  
• Holiness in Christ is not fragile — it is transformative.  
• True leadership absorbs cost rather than guarding image.  
• Compassion is strength directed outward.  

### Reflection

• Who do you instinctively keep distance from?  
• What fears shape your boundaries?  
• How would your leadership change if you saw compassion as authority?

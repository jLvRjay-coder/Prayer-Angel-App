# Episode 1 — Listening to the Wrong Voices

---

## Ages 6–9 — A King Who Listened to the Wrong People

### 🧭 Big Idea
Who you listen to shapes the kind of person you become.

### 📜 The Story
The city of Samaria was filled with noise the day Ahab became king. Trumpets sounded from the palace walls. Soldiers stood in shining armor. Servants hurried through the halls preparing for celebration.

A new king had taken the throne.

His name was Ahab.

Ahab had everything a king could want. He had power, wealth, influence, and a strong army. The people would look to him for direction. His voice would shape the nation.

But there was something even more important than his crown.

Every leader must decide whose voice matters most.

God had given Israel clear instructions. He had rescued them from Egypt. He had parted the Red Sea. He had given them His law so they would know how to live. The kings were supposed to lead the people in obedience to the Lord.

But Ahab chose differently.

He married a woman named Jezebel, who worshiped false gods. She did not honor the Lord, and she did not want Israel to honor Him either. Instead of guiding his kingdom toward truth, Ahab allowed her influence to grow.

Altars were built for idols.
False worship became normal.
The voice of God grew quieter in the land.

The Bible says Ahab did more evil in the sight of the Lord than all the kings before him (1 Kings 16:30–33, KJV).

Ahab was not forced to make these choices. He chose whose voice to follow.

And when a king listens to the wrong voices, a nation often follows.

God was still speaking.

But Ahab chose not to listen.

### 💡 What This Teaches (6–9)
- Who you listen to matters.
- God’s voice leads us in the right direction.
- Following the crowd is not always wise.
- Leaders must choose carefully whom they follow.

> ### 🪑 Think About It
> 1) Who do you listen to when you make decisions?
> 2) How can you know if advice is good or not?
> 3) How can you ask God to help you choose wisely?

## Ages 10–13 — Following the Wrong Advice

### 🧭 Big Idea
Compromise begins when we allow the wrong influence to shape us.

### 📜 The Story
Ahab became king during a fragile time in Israel’s history. The nation had already been drifting from wholehearted obedience to God. Instead of correcting that drift, Ahab accelerated it.

His marriage to Jezebel was not merely personal. It was political. Alliances between nations were often sealed through marriage, and Sidon was powerful. But what strengthened Israel politically weakened it spiritually.

Jezebel actively promoted the worship of Baal. She supported priests of false gods and encouraged idol worship throughout the land. Ahab did not resist her influence. In fact, he built an altar for Baal in Samaria (1 Kings 16:31–32, KJV).

The tragedy of Ahab’s reign is not that he lacked knowledge.

He knew Israel’s story.
He knew the covenant.
He knew what God required of a king.

But knowledge without obedience leads to erosion.

Compromise rarely appears as open rebellion. It begins with tolerance. It continues with accommodation. It ends with transformation of identity.

Ahab did not overthrow the worship of the Lord in one dramatic moment. He allowed other voices to share the stage.

But God does not share His glory.

Leadership is shaped by influence. When conviction weakens, compromise strengthens.

Ahab’s reign became a turning point in Israel’s decline because he listened to the wrong voices and allowed them to shape the nation.

### 💡 What This Teaches (10–13)
- Influence determines direction.
- Compromise grows gradually.
- Political strength cannot replace spiritual faithfulness.
- Courage means resisting pressure, even when it is costly.

> ### 🪑 Reflection
> 1) Who influences your thinking the most right now?
> 2) Are those voices drawing you toward God or away from Him?
> 3) Where might small compromises be shaping bigger outcomes?

## Young Adult / Adult — Leadership Shaped by Influence

### 🧭 Big Idea
Silent compromise reshapes nations before anyone notices.

### 📜 The Story
The biblical summary of Ahab’s reign is devastating: “Ahab… did evil in the sight of the Lord above all that were before him” (1 Kings 16:30, KJV).

This statement is not hyperbole. It is theological assessment.

Ahab’s failure was not impulsive rebellion. It was calculated accommodation. His marriage to Jezebel strengthened international ties but fractured covenant identity. What appeared politically strategic proved spiritually catastrophic.

Idolatry became institutional.
False worship gained royal endorsement.
Prophets of the Lord were marginalized.

Compromise rarely feels dramatic at first. It feels pragmatic. It feels reasonable. It often feels necessary.

But leadership magnifies influence. What a king tolerates, a nation normalizes.

Ahab’s reign demonstrates a sobering truth: the most dangerous shift in a culture does not occur when evil storms the gates. It occurs when truth quietly loses priority.

God sent prophets.
God gave warnings.
God remained faithful to His covenant.

But humility is required to listen.

Ahab’s tragedy was not that God stopped speaking.

It was that he preferred other voices.

Leadership is always shaped by allegiance. When approval, ambition, or comfort outweigh obedience, decline begins.

The drift of Israel under Ahab did not happen in a single day. It unfolded decision by decision, silence by silence.

And nations often follow the voice their leaders choose to trust.

### 💡 What This Teaches (Adult)
- Compromise is progressive before it is visible.
- Political wisdom without spiritual fidelity leads to decline.
- Influence is never neutral.
- Humility determines whether we respond to correction.

> ### 🪑 Reflection
> 1) Whose voice carries the greatest authority in your life?
> 2) Where might strategic decisions be masking spiritual compromise?
> 3) Are you guarding conviction, or gradually negotiating it?
